How to Read Deeply in a Distracted Age

Recovering the capacity for immersive, absorbed reading in a world designed to fragment attention.

Deep reading — the kind of immersive, focused engagement with a text that allows its ideas to genuinely reshape your thinking — has become increasingly rare. The same cognitive circuits that sustain it are continuously recruited by notifications, social media, and the ambient pull of digital distraction. Recovering the capacity requires deliberate effort.

The practical interventions are straightforward but require commitment: a dedicated reading space with the phone physically absent; a consistent reading time that becomes a protected habit; physical books rather than screen-based reading where possible. Research by Adrian Ward at the University of Texas found that the mere presence of a smartphone on a desk, even face-down and silent, significantly reduces available cognitive capacity. The return on investing in deep reading is not merely information — it is the training of a kind of attention that makes everything else richer and more penetrating.

The Art of Research: Finding Truth in the Information Age

A practical guide to evaluating sources, spotting bias, and building reliable knowledge.

The internet has made access to information essentially free and essentially universal. It has not made the evaluation of information easier. The abundance of sources — many designed to appear credible, many operating with undisclosed interests — has made the job of the careful reader considerably harder.

Primary sources are the foundation of reliable knowledge. A claim that a study found something is always weaker than the study itself. Lateral reading — immediately checking what other sources say about a publisher before reading the content — has been shown to be significantly more effective than trying to evaluate content on its internal merits alone. Understanding the incentive structure of sources is equally important: who published this, and what do they gain from it performing well? These questions don't determine whether information is true or false, but provide essential context for evaluating it.

Why Non-Fiction Books Still Matter More Than Ever

What the long-form book does that no other medium can replicate.

The long-form non-fiction book is an endangered medium — or so we are frequently told. This argument consistently underestimates what the book does that no other medium replicates. It is not a delivery mechanism for information. It is a training ground for a specific kind of thinking — sustained, linear, cumulative, argument-following thought that builds understanding over time rather than delivering impressions in fragments.

A well-written non-fiction book makes an extended argument: a sustained case, a sequence of claims each resting on those before it, building toward a conclusion that cannot be reached any faster without being reached less securely. In a world saturated with information, the scarce resource is not access to facts but the capacity to organise facts into coherent understanding — to see the structure beneath the surface. That capacity is exactly what sustained reading develops. The book is not competing with the podcast or the video essay. It is doing something those formats cannot do.
